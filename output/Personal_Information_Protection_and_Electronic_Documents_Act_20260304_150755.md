# Canadian Legal Research Package: Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act (PIPEDA)

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act (PIPEDA) is Canada's federal private sector privacy law that governs how organizations collect, use, and disclose personal information during commercial activities. The Act establishes privacy rights for individuals, including the right to access their personal information, and sets out ten fair information principles (contained in Schedule 1) that organizations must follow. PIPEDA also creates an oversight regime through the Privacy Commissioner of Canada, who investigates complaints, conducts audits, and can refer matters to the Federal Court for enforcement.

## 4. KEY OBLIGATIONS AND PROHIBITIONS

### Organizational Obligations

**General Compliance (s. 5)**
- Organizations **must** comply with the 10 fair information principles in Schedule 1
- Organizations **may only** collect, use, or disclose personal information for purposes a reasonable person would consider appropriate

**Consent Requirements (ss. 6.1, 7)**
- Organizations **must** obtain valid consent that a reasonable person would understand
- Consent is only valid if the individual understands the nature, purpose, and consequences of the collection, use, or disclosure

**Access Requests (s. 8)**
- Organizations **must** respond to access requests within 30 days
- Organizations **must** assist individuals who need help preparing requests
- Organizations **may** extend the time limit by a maximum of 30 days (with notice) if meeting the deadline would unreasonably interfere with activities
- Organizations **must** retain personal information subject to a request long enough for the individual to exhaust recourse options
- Organizations **may** charge costs only after informing the individual of approximate cost and confirming they wish to proceed

**Breach of Security Safeguards (ss. 10.1-10.3)**
- Organizations **must** report to the Commissioner any breach creating a "real risk of significant harm"
- Organizations **must** notify affected individuals of qualifying breaches
- Organizations **must** notify other organizations/government institutions if they may be able to reduce harm
- Organizations **must** keep and maintain records of all breaches
- Organizations **must** provide breach records to the Commissioner on request

**Record Keeping**
- Organizations **must** maintain breach records in accordance with prescribed requirements (s. 10.3)

### Permitted Activities Without Consent

**Collection Without Consent (s. 7(1))** - Permitted when:
- Collection is clearly in individual's interest and consent cannot be timely obtained
- Consent would compromise availability/accuracy of information for breach/law investigations
- Information is in a witness statement for insurance claims
- Information was produced in course of employment/business/profession
- Collection is for journalistic, artistic, or literary purposes
- Information is publicly available (as specified in regulations)
- Collection is for making certain disclosures

**Use Without Consent (s. 7(2))** - Permitted when:
- Organization discovers information useful for investigating law contraventions
- Use is for emergency threatening life, health, or security
- Information is in witness statements for insurance claims
- Information was produced in employment/business/profession context
- For statistical/scholarly research (with confidentiality and Commissioner notification)
- Information is publicly available (as specified in regulations)

**Disclosure Without Consent (s. 7(3))** - Permitted when:
- To legal counsel representing the organization
- For debt collection
- To comply with subpoenas, warrants, court orders
- To government institutions with lawful authority (various purposes)
- To FINTRAC as required by law
- When organization suspects law contravention or national security matter
- To other organizations for fraud detection/prevention
- For emergencies threatening life, health, or security
- For statistical/scholarly research (with conditions)
- To archival institutions
- 100+ years after record creation or 20+ years after individual's death
- As required by law

**Business Transactions (s. 7.2)** - Organizations may use/disclose without consent:
- During prospective transactions (with confidentiality agreements)
- After completed transactions (with ongoing protections and individual notification)

**Employment Relationships (s. 7.3)** - Federal works/undertakings may collect, use, disclose without consent:
- To establish, manage, or terminate employment
- If individual is informed of the potential collection/use/disclosure

### Prohibitions

**Access Restrictions (s. 9)**
- Organizations **shall not** give access if it would reveal third-party personal information (unless severable or consent obtained)
- Organizations **shall not** comply with access requests if government institution objects on national security/law enforcement grounds

**Electronic Address Collection (s. 7.1)**
- Organizations **cannot** rely on consent exceptions when collecting electronic addresses using programs designed primarily to harvest addresses
- Organizations **cannot** rely on consent exceptions when collecting personal information by unauthorized access to computer systems

**General Prohibitions**
- Organizations **shall not** collect, use, or disclose personal information for purposes a reasonable person would not consider appropriate (s. 5(3))

## 5. PENALTIES AND ENFORCEMENT

### Oversight Structure

**Privacy Commissioner (s. 2(1))**
- The Privacy Commissioner appointed under the Privacy Act oversees PIPEDA
- Can investigate complaints (s. 12)
- Can initiate complaints on own motion (s. 11(2))
- Issues reports with findings and recommendations (s. 13)

### Complaint Process

**Filing Complaints (s. 11)**
- Individuals may file written complaints for contraventions of Division 1 or 1.1, or for not following Schedule 1 recommendations
- Complaints about access refusals must be filed within 6 months
- Commissioner may initiate complaints independently

**Investigation Powers (s. 12.1)**
- Summon witnesses and compel testimony under oath
- Compel production of records
- Enter premises (other than dwellings) and examine records
- Mediation and conciliation available

**Commissioner's Report (s. 13)**
- Must be prepared within one year of complaint
- Contains findings, recommendations, settlements, and recourse options
- May request organization report on implementation actions

### Court Remedies (s. 14 - partial)

**Federal Court Application**
- Complainants may apply to Federal Court after receiving Commissioner's report or discontinuance notice
- Court has jurisdiction over matters in the complaint or Commissioner's report

### Offences and Penalties

**Note:** The provided text was truncated before reaching the penalties section. Based on PIPEDA's structure, the following penalties exist (from sections not provided):

- **Obstruction offence**: Up to $10,000 fine (summary conviction) or $100,000 fine (indictment) for obstructing the Commissioner
- **Breach notification offences**: Up to $100,000 fine for:
  - Failing to report breaches to Commissioner
  - Failing to notify affected individuals
  - Failing to maintain breach records
  - Knowingly contravening breach notification requirements
- **Retaliation prohibition**: Offence to dismiss, discipline, or penalize employees for reporting PIPEDA contraventions

### Compliance Agreements (s. 17.1 - referenced but not provided)

The Act provides for compliance agreements as an enforcement tool, referenced in s. 12.2(1)(c.1).

### Ambiguities and Interpretive Issues

1. **"Reasonable Person" Standard (s. 5(3))**: The "appropriate purposes" test relies on a reasonable person standard that requires case-by-case assessment

2. **"Real Risk of Significant Harm" (s. 10.1)**: While factors are listed (s. 10.1(8)), this threshold requires judgment calls - Commissioner guidance would be valuable

3. **"Due Diligence" (s. 8(3))**: The 30-day response period is subject to "due diligence" - interpretation may vary

4. **Valid Consent (s. 6.1)**: The test references what an individual "to whom the organization's activities are directed would understand" - this population-based approach may create interpretive challenges

### Areas Requiring Further Research

1. **Provincial Substantially Similar Legislation**: PIPEDA doesn't apply in provinces with "substantially similar" legislation (Alberta, British Columbia, Quebec) - research needed on which provincial laws are deemed substantially similar and interaction with PIPEDA

2. **Regulatory Framework**: The Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Regulations would clarify:
   - What constitutes "publicly available" information
   - Breach reporting form and manner requirements
   - Other prescribed factors

3. **Commissioner Guidance**: Office of the Privacy Commissioner guidelines, findings, and interpretations would provide practical compliance guidance

4. **Case Law**: Federal Court decisions interpreting PIPEDA provisions would clarify ambiguous terms

5. **Recent Amendments**: The Act shows amendments up to March 4, 2025 - recent changes (particularly from Bill C-27 discussions) should be verified

6. **Health Information**: PIPEDA's application to personal health information varies by province - provincial health privacy laws may apply instead

7. **Cross-Border Data Transfers**: Schedule 1 principles address transfers to third parties but specific rules for international transfers require further analysis

8. **Employee Information**: PIPEDA's application to employee information is limited to federal works, undertakings, and businesses - provincial laws govern most employee privacy
