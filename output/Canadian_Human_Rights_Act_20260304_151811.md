# CANADIAN LEGAL RESEARCH PACKAGE
## Canadian Human Rights Act (RSC 1985, c H-6)

---

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Canadian Human Rights Act is federal legislation that prohibits discrimination based on protected grounds (such as race, sex, disability, religion, and others) in areas of federal jurisdiction, including employment, services, and accommodation. It establishes the Canadian Human Rights Commission to investigate complaints, the Canadian Human Rights Tribunal to adjudicate disputes, and provides remedies for victims of discriminatory practices. The Act aims to ensure all individuals have equal opportunity to make the lives they wish without being hindered by discriminatory practices.

## 4. KEY OBLIGATIONS AND PROHIBITIONS

### A. Prohibited Grounds of Discrimination (s. 3)
The following are protected characteristics:
- Race
- National or ethnic origin
- Colour
- Religion
- Age
- Sex (including pregnancy and childbirth – s. 3(2))
- Sexual orientation
- Gender identity or expression
- Marital status
- Family status
- Genetic characteristics (including refusal to undergo genetic testing – s. 3(3))
- Disability
- Conviction for which a pardon/record suspension has been granted

**Note:** Discrimination on multiple/combined grounds is explicitly prohibited (s. 3.1)

### B. Discriminatory Practices Prohibited

| Section | Prohibited Conduct | Context |
|---------|-------------------|---------|
| s. 5 | Denial of goods, services, facilities, or accommodation; adverse differentiation | Public services |
| s. 6 | Denial of commercial premises or residential accommodation | Housing |
| s. 7 | Refusal to employ/continue employment; adverse differentiation in employment | Employment |
| s. 8 | Discriminatory employment applications or advertisements | Hiring |
| s. 9 | Exclusion, expulsion, suspension from employee organizations; limiting employment opportunities | Unions |
| s. 10 | Discriminatory policies or agreements affecting employment | Employers, unions, employer organizations |
| s. 11 | Wage differences between male and female employees for work of equal value | Equal pay |
| s. 12 | Publishing discriminatory notices, signs, symbols | Public communications |
| s. 14 | Harassment (including sexual harassment) | Services, housing, employment |
| s. 14.1 | Retaliation against complainants | Post-complaint conduct |

### C. Equal Pay Obligations (s. 11)

**Employers MUST:**
- Pay equal wages to male and female employees performing work of equal value in the same establishment
- Assess value using composite of: skill, effort, responsibility, and working conditions
- NOT reduce wages to eliminate pay gaps

**"Wages" includes (s. 11(7)):**
- Salaries, commissions, vacation pay, dismissal wages, bonuses
- Value of board, rent, housing, lodging
- Payments in kind
- Employer pension and insurance contributions
- Other direct/indirect advantages from employer

### D. Permitted Exceptions (s. 15)

Discrimination is NOT prohibited where:
| Exception | Conditions |
|-----------|------------|
| Bona fide occupational requirement | Must show accommodation would cause undue hardship (considering health, safety, cost) |
| Age limits | Minimum/maximum age established by law or regulations |
| Pension vesting requirements | Compliant with Pension Benefits Standards Act |
| Commission guidelines | Practice conforms to CHRC "reasonable" guidelines |
| Pregnancy/parental leave | Special leave or benefits for pregnancy, childbirth, or childcare |
| Bona fide justification | Denial of services/accommodation with legitimate justification and undue hardship shown |

**Key Principle (s. 15(2)):** For any exception to apply, the person must establish that accommodation would impose **undue hardship** considering:
- Health
- Safety
- Cost

### E. Special Programs Permitted (s. 16)
- Programs designed to prevent, eliminate, or reduce disadvantages suffered by groups based on prohibited grounds are NOT discriminatory
- Collection of information about prohibited grounds for such programs is permitted

### F. Disability Accommodation Plans (ss. 17-19)
- Organizations may apply to CHRC for approval of disability accommodation plans
- Approved plans provide defence against complaints
- Commission may rescind approval if circumstances change
- Plans must accommodate needs; variance from accessibility standards alone doesn't make plan inappropriate

### G. Commission Obligations (s. 27)

The Canadian Human Rights Commission MUST:
- Develop public information programs
- Sponsor research on human rights
- Maintain liaison with provincial bodies
- Try to discourage discriminatory practices through persuasion and publicity

The Commission MAY:
- Issue binding guidelines on Act interpretation
- Review regulations and instruments for discrimination
- Consider recommendations on human rights issues

### H. Confidentiality Obligations (s. 33)

Commission members and employees MUST:
- Comply with security requirements for sensitive information
- Avoid disclosing matters that could harm:
  - International relations, national defence, security
  - Federal-provincial relations
  - Cabinet confidences
  - Criminal investigations
  - Prisoner rehabilitation
  - Court/tribunal proceedings
  - Legal privilege

## 5. PENALTIES AND ENFORCEMENT

### A. Complaint Process (Part III - partial)

**Who May Complain (s. 40):**
- Any individual or group with reasonable grounds to believe discrimination occurred
- Commission may initiate complaints on its own motion (but not based on Employment Equity Act audit information)
- Multiple complaints on same issues may be joined

**Jurisdictional Requirements (s. 40(5)):**
The act/omission must have:
- Occurred in Canada (victim lawfully present or entitled to return), OR
- Occurred in Canada (systemic discrimination with no identifiable victim), OR
- Occurred outside Canada (victim was Canadian citizen or permanent resident)

### B. Limitations on Commission Jurisdiction

| Limitation | Section | Effect |
|------------|---------|--------|
| Pension/superannuation rights pre-March 1, 1978 | s. 40(7) | Cannot order remedy that removes pre-existing rights |
| Employment equity statistics alone | s. 40.1 | Cannot base complaint solely on underrepresentation statistics |
| Pay Equity Act matters | s. 40.2 | Commission has no jurisdiction over s. 7, 10, 11 complaints against Pay Equity Act employers regarding equal pay |

### B. Key Areas Requiring Further Research

1. **Remedial Powers (s. 53):** What specific orders can the Tribunal make? Compensation limits? Systemic remedies available?

2. **Offences (s. 60):** Are there criminal penalties? What conduct triggers them?

3. **Investigation Powers (ss. 43-47):** What are the Commission's investigative authorities? Subpoena power? Entry/search?

4. **Tribunal Procedures (ss. 48.1-49):** Hearing procedures, evidence rules, judicial review rights?

5. **Limitation Periods:** Are there time limits for filing complaints?

6. **Federal Court Review:** What is the standard of review for Tribunal decisions?

### C. Interpretive Considerations

1. **"Undue Hardship" Standard (s. 15(2)):** The Act identifies factors (health, safety, cost) but Governor in Council may make regulations prescribing standards. Need to check if such regulations exist.

2. **Guidelines (s. 27(2)):** CHRC guidelines are binding on Commission and Tribunal panels. Need to identify current guidelines in force.

3. **Interaction with Pay Equity Act:** Section 40.2 creates significant jurisdictional exclusion. For employers under Pay Equity Act, equal pay complaints now go through that statute, not CHRA.

4. **Interaction with Accessible Canada Act:** Accessibility Commissioner has dual role; s. 40.01 permits information sharing between complaint files and Accessibility Commissioner.

### D. Ambiguities Identified

1. **"Services customarily available to the general public" (s. 5):** What is the threshold for "customarily available"? Private clubs? Membership organizations?

2. **"Bona fide occupational requirement" vs. "bona fide justification" (s. 15):** Different language used for employment (para a) vs. services (para g) - is the standard different?

3. **Genetic Characteristics (s. 3(3)):** Applies to refusal to undergo testing OR refusal to disclose results - but what about voluntary disclosure that leads to adverse treatment?

4. **Multiple/Combined Grounds (s. 3.1):** "Intersectional" discrimination is covered, but how is this applied in remedy calculations?
